# After AI Takes Over Execution — A New Basic Social Architecture

Author: @v5ria WK

> "Decentralized Society Trilogy" · Part 3 · Endgame / Why It Becomes Necessary in the AI Era

I've been thinking on two threads at the same time lately. One is decentralization and "distributing observation rights" (the first two pieces — [ontology](./Observation-Now-Decides-the-Past.md) / [methodology](./Distributing-Observation-Rights.md) — already covered this). The other is the AI work I do every day — long-horizon tasks, automated execution, automated understanding, automated decision-making.

At first I thought they were two things. Then I realized: **these two threads have to be connected, otherwise neither one works on its own.**

This piece is written for myself: thinking the endgame of "AI replacing execution" all the way through, and then seeing what role decentralization actually plays in that endgame.

## AI is already replacing cognitive labor; physical labor is just a matter of time

My day-to-day work makes it hard for me to pretend there's still suspense about this.

Writing code, writing copy, doing analysis, making decisions — these used to be considered "high-end cognitive work." They're being replaced by AI faster than most people expect. The long-horizon tasks running at our company — one Agent can do in two days what used to take a small team a month. Once that has happened to you once, you can't go back.

Physical labor is only slightly later. Embodied intelligence, the cost curve of robotics, the maturation of perception models — every indicator points the same direction: **in the next five to fifteen years, the vast majority of structurable labor, cognitive or physical, will be covered by automation**.

This isn't a prediction. It's the trend line extended forward. When execution (cognitive or physical) can almost all be done by AI, a sharp question gets put in front of every social architecture:

**If labor is no longer how most people create value, where does value flow? What does the human still have inside the production system?**

## The default future is bad

If nothing changes, the default script reads like this:

- The productivity gains from AI accrue to a small number of centralized institutions that own the underlying models, compute, and data
- Most people lose economic bargaining power, because they neither own the AI nor are needed by it
- Wealth concentration breaks past historical peaks, social stratification hardens

This isn't what some villain wants. It's what the structure produces. **When "production" can detach from "humans," there is no mechanism guaranteeing that production's results return to human hands — unless that mechanism is deliberately designed.**

In the industrial revolution's story, machines replaced human muscle, but humans still held knowledge work and could still bargain. The AI revolution's story is different: **when machines replace muscle and knowledge work at the same time, humans have no chips left to bargain with**. Unless we mint new chips.

## What humans have left isn't labor — it's "collapse"

But this script isn't the only possibility.

Once AI takes over the "execution" layer, humans don't lose value — human value just migrates from the execution layer to the **intent layer, the observation layer, the meaning layer**.

Breaking these three apart:

**1. Intent (what is worth doing)**

AI can execute infinitely efficiently, but it doesn't know "what is worth executing." Whether something is worth doing has never been a technical question — it's a value judgment. An Agent can write ten thousand lines of code in a day, but deciding "what to write, for whom, solving what problem for whom" still depends on humans — and **the closer the human is to the end user, the better they understand**.

The granularity of intent determines whether AI can actually solve concrete problems. This is naturally distributed across every concrete person with a real need.

**2. Observation (the collapse of fact)**

AI can produce results, but **whether the result is "good," "right," "actually solved the problem" needs an observer to collapse**.

This isn't a single-point sign-off. It's a distributed, continuous observation process completed jointly by everyone affected. The "distributing observation rights" from the previous piece becomes more important in the AI era, not less — because AI's output volume is exponential, **a single centralized review can't possibly keep up**, and it has to be observed jointly by everyone relevant.

**3. Meaning (why this matters)**

AI can tell you what's most efficient, but it can't tell you why to be alive, what kind of life is good, what kind of society is worthwhile. This part has to be left to humans, and must always be left to humans. Meaning isn't a computable optimization target — it's a judgment that grows out of lived experience.

These three together make up the core human productivity of the AI era:

> **Not labor — collapse. Not execution — participation.**

## Decentralization is the load-bearing wall of the new social architecture

By here, why decentralization becomes necessary almost emerges on its own.

Intent, observation, meaning — these three things are **inherently distributed across every concrete person**. They cannot, and should not, be monopolized by any centralized institution:

- If **intent** is monopolized by one company → AI is only used to produce what that company considers important
- If **observation** is monopolized by one platform → whether AI's output is "good" is decided by that platform
- If **meaning** is monopolized by one narrative → society's whole sense of direction gets locked into a single interpretation

Any of these monopolies will make AI, this super-production-machine, sprint at high speed in the wrong direction. **And historically, the cost of sprinting at high speed in the wrong direction has no upper bound.**

So the AI era must be paired with a decentralized social substrate. Not as a rebellion against anyone, but because **distributed human input naturally needs a distributed catching structure**.

What this substrate has to do is actually quite concrete:

- **An on-chain proof-of-contribution system**: every person's intent input, observation feedback, value judgment for an AI system gets recorded as quantifiable contribution
- **Contribution-based value distribution**: the economic value AI creates flows by contribution to everyone who actually participated in "collapse," instead of only to the owners of compute and models
- **Composable collaboration units**: a single person + Agent group forms an OPC; multiple OPCs assemble temporarily into mission squads, disband afterward, but the contributions all stay on-chain and auto-settle
- **A non-monopolizable protocol layer**: the substrate belongs to no single company; it's maintained jointly by all nodes

The hardest thing about this system isn't technology — it's **getting most people to realize they have a "collapse-asset"**. Your judgment, your viewpoint, your real feedback — in the AI era, these can be priced, **provided you're willing to feed them into a protocol that records contribution, instead of giving them away to any free centralized platform**.

## How the basic social architecture stratifies

Putting all this together, the future basic social architecture roughly becomes four layers:

**Layer 1: Execution layer (AI)**  
The structurable labor — cognitive plus physical — done by Agents, Skills, robots, automation systems. The defining trait of this layer: infinitely scalable, near-zero marginal cost.

**Layer 2: Observation & verification layer (humans + AI cross-verifying)**  
Distributed humans serve as observation nodes, collapsing, correcting, and validating AI's output. AIs also cross-verify each other, but **the final "is this actually valuable" must be collapsed by humans**. This layer is the new society's "quality assurance system."

**Layer 3: Value-distribution layer (decentralized protocols)**  
All intent input, observation contribution, and judgment decision get recorded on-chain; the economic value AI creates is auto-distributed by contribution. This layer replaces today's "wages — equity — taxes" system as the new wealth-circulation mechanism.

**Layer 4: Meaning & direction layer (humans)**  
Where should society go? What kind of life is worth living? What kind of progress is real progress? This layer is left entirely to humans, and must be jointly collapsed by as many humans as possible — otherwise it's a few people deciding direction for everyone.

The relationship between these four layers: the execution layer infinitely amplifies productivity, the meaning layer defines direction, **and the middle two layers (observation + value distribution) decide whether the amplified output actually serves that direction**.

**The middle two layers are exactly the position decentralization has to fill. It's the load-bearing wall of the new social architecture.**

## The transition window is the most dangerous, and the most critical

We're now in an extremely dangerous window:

- AI is taking over the execution layer faster than expected
- But the decentralized infrastructure that's supposed to catch the value spillover from AI hasn't taken shape yet
- In this gap, all productivity gains are concentrating into the centralized AI owners

This window may only be five to ten years. **If the decentralized value-distribution system doesn't get built within this window, the wealth structure of the AI era will lock into a state of extreme concentration** — and after that, restructuring it will be very difficult, possibly so difficult it requires violent social conflict to complete.

So if you ask me for a verdict:

> **The most important engineering problem of the next ten years isn't making AI stronger — it's building the decentralized infrastructure that lets the value AI creates flow back to all the "collapse nodes."**

Making the model stronger is something for smart people. **Getting the distribution mechanism right is something that has to happen.** The first has trillions of dollars of capital pushing it. The second barely has anyone taking it seriously.

This asymmetry is itself the loudest danger signal of this era.

## So as an ordinary person, what can I do right now

After writing all that big architecture, I owe myself an answer to a smaller, sharper question: **if I'm not at an AI company, not a protocol designer, not a policymaker, what can I do today?**

I thought about it. Probably these five things:

**1. Stop giving away your "collapse-assets" to centralized platforms for free**

Every review you write on some app, every rating you give, every piece of feedback — fundamentally you're feeding your judgment, for free, to a centralized collapser. It collects this data, integrates it, makes it its asset, then sells the integrated version back to you. **You did the observation work, but not a single observation belongs to you.**

Once you see this, at the very least, start being selective with your output — leave the judgments you actually thought through for systems that might record contribution and flow value back, even if they're rough today.

**2. Start exercising the muscle of "independent observation"**

The comfort of the centralized era is not having to observe — the platform already filtered for you. But once that muscle atrophies, when the moment comes that you actually need to be an observation node, you can't generate force.

How to train it? My own approach: **when I encounter a widely circulated "fact," I first ask "have I personally verified this?"** If not, I tag it as "second-hand" and don't easily forward it or use it as a basis for judgment. It feels counterintuitive at first; do it long enough and you'll find your understanding of the world drops one order of magnitude in granularity.

**3. Settle your sharpest "collapse capability" into a permanent record**

Everyone has at least one thing they see more accurately than others — could be food, code quality, judging people, evaluating a piece of music or writing or clothing. **This is your capital in the AI era.**

Record it continuously. Even if there's no protocol today to cash it in, build the continuous observation track first. When the catching system matures, the long-accumulated judgment will have a nonlinear cash-in window.

**4. Join or create a small-scale collaboration unit**

You don't have to wait for a big DAO. Start with a few people around you. A small project, a co-creation community, an OPC seedling — any small-scale collaboration where **contribution gets seen, value flows back** is practice for the new society's collaboration grammar.

The industrial era's collaboration grammar is "hierarchy + wages." The new collaboration grammar is "proof of contribution + automatic settlement." This switch can't be completed by reading a few articles — it has to be done.

**5. Guard your own clarity at the meaning layer**

Last and possibly most important: **don't outsource the question "what is worth doing" to AI, algorithms, or any centralized narrative.**

AI will get better and better at telling you how to do things most efficiently. It should never be allowed to tell you why to be alive. This is the human's last and deepest moat. Guard it, and you have the right to be a clear-eyed collapse node in the new architecture; surrender it, and no amount of on-chain proof of contribution will save you.

## Closing: looping back to Wheeler

After a long detour I land back on Wheeler again.

Wheeler said the universe is "participatory" — without observers, the universe doesn't really exist.

The society of the AI era will, in a strange way, re-confirm this: **once AI takes over most of "execution," what humans have left is "participation" — observation, intent, collapse, judgment**. This was always the human's deepest capacity; it had just been buried by centuries of industrialization and repetitive work.

Which is why I'm not as pessimistic about "AI replacing humans" as many people are. What it's replacing is, precisely, **the part of the human that was never supposed to be locked there**.

But there's a precondition: **we have to build the other social architecture — the one that catches human participation — before AI replaces things more thoroughly**.

That architecture's name is decentralization.

It isn't an ideological choice. It's the engineering question of whether the AI era's society can soft-land.

The stakes here are bigger than most of us realize.
