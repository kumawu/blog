After finishing [the previous piece](./Observation-Now-Decides-the-Past.md), I kept asking myself one question: **if "distributing observation rights" stays at the philosophical level, it's just a pretty phrase.**

So this one isn't about why — it's about what it looks like. I've been chewing on a few concrete landing scenarios — blue-collar recruiting, the livestreaming ecosystem, internal company organization, the AI-era one-person company (OPC). The industries are wildly different, but the further I dig, the more I feel they share **the same underlying structural transformation**:

> **From "one observer collapses fact for everyone" to "all participants collapse fact together."**

Below, four scenarios, each viewed through this lens. The point isn't an industry blueprint — it's **what shape this transformation actually takes**.

## Scenario 1: Blue-collar recruiting — taking "trust" out of one company's hands

The biggest pain point in blue-collar recruiting has never been a shortage of workers or jobs. It's that **the cost of trust is enormous**.

Workers don't know whether the boss will hold back wages. Bosses don't know whether the worker will quit after two days. Middlemen sit between the two, taking money from both sides while answering to neither. Everyone is guessing, everyone is afraid of getting screwed.

Why? Because in this ecosystem, **"what happened in the past" has no public record**. Which sites this worker has been on, whether he showed up on time, whether he was easy to work with — only a scattered handful of bosses and foremen know, and they have neither incentive nor channel to share. Every new match starts trust from zero, and so the platform/middleman gets the rent-extraction zone — they become the sole "trust observer," living off the information asymmetry.

The decentralized solution isn't building a bigger platform. It's **turning every actual employment relationship into a publicly verifiable observation event**: arrival time, completion quality, whether settlement was on time, mutual evaluations — written in by participants, made tamper-proof by the protocol.

Once "the past" becomes something everyone can see, the cost of trust drops off a cliff and matching efficiency rises naturally. **The platform stops being the source of trust and becomes the infrastructure for observation.**

The deepest shift here is: the "résumé" of a worker or a boss used to be a version collapsed by a few middlemen. Now it becomes a composite collapsed by hundreds of real employment relationships. **No single node can define who you are, but the sum of all nodes' observations will be more accurate than any middleman ever could be.**

## Scenario 2: The livestreaming ecosystem — the value-distribution "black box" should be opened

What's interesting about the livestreaming ecosystem is that it's fundamentally a **multi-role collaborative value-creation** scene: streamers, top patrons (大哥大姐), guilds (工会), the platform — none of them dispensable. But the value-distribution structure is highly centralized — the platform decides where traffic goes, what the cut is, who gets recommended, what counts as "violation."

Everyone is creating value, but **only one observer is collapsing "who created how much."**

In my view, this ecosystem is the most natural candidate for being redone with decentralized mechanisms. Each role's contribution is in fact observable and quantifiable:

- A top patron's tip isn't just a one-time payment — it's **a vote on the streamer's worth**, and that vote itself is shaping the streamer's growth trajectory.
- The guild's operational investment, training investment, and traffic referrals are another structured contribution.
- What the platform provides is no longer "the central algorithm that decides everything," but **a protocol layer that lets multi-party observation be recorded and settled**.

If all these contributions can be recorded on-chain and auto-settled by rule, a new shape emerges in the ecosystem: **the streamer's growth is collapsed jointly by everyone who has supported them, instead of being unilaterally defined by the platform's traffic-allocation mechanism**.

Value distribution shifts from "black box" to "open books." The platform's role retreats from "referee + player" back to "groundskeeper." This isn't a moral favor — it's structurally more stable, because **the more observers participate in collapse, the harder it is for any one party to distort the fact**.

## Scenario 3: Internal company organization — breaking year-end review into daily micro-observations

The third scenario hits me personally, because it's happening inside the environment I work in every day.

The core problem of traditional company organization is: **a person's value over a year is collapsed by their direct manager, in the few weeks around year-end**.

This is an extremely centralized observation structure. The manager's memory, preferences, current mood, and impressions of the most recent three months of projects almost entirely determine what "happened" in this person's year. The work you did in the first nine months, if it didn't leave a trace in the manager's head, might as well have never happened.

This is structurally and astonishingly similar to Wheeler's delayed-choice experiment — **the parts that weren't collapsed by that specific observer never really "existed."**

The essence of a points-based system isn't "more bonuses." It's **breaking single-observer-plus-once-a-year collapse into multi-observer-plus-continuous-micro-collapse**:

- You helped a colleague solve a problem — the colleague can log it
- A doc you wrote was cited by three people — the system can log it
- You proposed an adopted solution in a cross-team meeting — participants can log it
- Your day-to-day expression, collaboration data, output quality — all observable independently by multiple parties

Each entry is small, but added up, **a person's value in the org no longer depends on one manager's memory — it's collapsed by dozens or hundreds of independent observation nodes**.

The biggest change isn't "fairer" — fairness is too light a word. The change is that **contributions that would otherwise have been forgotten by year-end now have the right to "really have happened."**

If this works, the shape of the organization slowly shifts from a tree to a network. It's not that there are no managers — it's that managers are no longer the sole collapsers of fact.

## Scenario 4: OPC and the AI workstation — even a one-person company can have a complete on-chain résumé

The last scenario is one I keep thinking about: once AI pushes productivity to its limit, what happens to the very form of "company"?

I believe a wave of "one-person companies" (OPC, one-person company) will emerge — one person plus a set of Agent + Skill can complete what used to require a team of a dozen. The Agent is the brain, Skills are the hands and feet, tokenized compute is the fuel.

What's interesting about this model isn't "saving money." It's that **it's naturally suited for redefining "contribution" and "value" in a decentralized way**.

Because in a one-person company, every line of code, every hardened Skill, every capability invocation is **an on-chain observable event**. Which means:

- What someone built solo no longer needs résumés and interviews to prove — their on-chain history is the hardest evidence.
- A few OPCs forming a temporary "mission squad" to deliver a project — each person's actual contribution is quantifiable, **revenue split can be written into the protocol instead of into a slide deck**.
- After the project ends and the squad disbands, each person's "proof of contribution" stays on-chain, automatically carrying over into the next collaboration.

This is decentralization's deepest impact on future organizational structure: **the company as legal entity may still exist, but "who created the value, who the value belongs to" no longer needs the company shell to do the collapsing**.

The company degrades from "the collapser of value" to "a temporary container for collaboration." That's no small paradigm shift.

## Pulling the camera back: the great-maintenance era is the bigger proposition

Here I want to pause and pull the camera back from these four specific industries.

Because the more I think about it, the more I feel these four examples are just the tip of the iceberg. **There are massive structurally identical, but bigger and more urgent scenarios in our society** waiting for a distribute-observation-rights solution.

The one that haunts me most is the **"great-maintenance era"** that China is entering.

Over the past three or four decades, we walked through humanity's most concentrated "great-construction" era: highways, high-speed rail, bridges, ports, power grids, water infrastructure, urban buildings, data centers. This was done in an extremely centralized way — national projects, state-owned enterprise execution, local landing. As a system for building assets, it was genius-tier.

But once it's built, the question becomes: **how do you maintain assets at this scale?**

A hidden crack in a bridge, corrosion in a stretch of pipe, structural fatigue in an old building, subsidence in a railbed — these don't wait for an inspection date. They're happening every day in every corner. To find and fix them in time, what's needed is **continuous, ubiquitous observation**.

And that kind of observation, **no centralized system can do**. Not because the system isn't trying, but because **the surface area of the problem is too large — it exceeds the physical limit of any single observer**. A province has tens of thousands of bridges and tunnels; a city has hundreds of thousands of kilometers of underground pipes — no department, however large, can watch them all. The result is that most hidden hazards are only discovered after an accident, by which time it's too late.

This is exactly what distributing observation rights solves:

- Every maintenance worker, every commuter, every resident, every inspection drone, every IoT sensor is an observation node
- Every "I see a crack here," "this road is sinking," "this building has strange noises" gets recorded as a verifiable observation event
- People who actually provide effective observation — professional or just passing by — are incentivized through on-chain proof of contribution
- An asset's "state" is no longer a number on a ledger updated once a year — **it's a live view jointly collapsed, continuously, by millions of observers**

This is an order of magnitude bigger than the four scenarios above. It's not a commercial project — it's a national-scale proposition. **And structurally it can almost only be solved by decentralization, because no centralized institution has enough "detector density" to cover it.**

## How many other scenarios are like this

Once you open this lens, you find this kind of "problem-surface-area-exceeds-the-limit-of-centralized-capacity" scenario everywhere around us:

- **Home eldercare in an aging society**: the real condition of hundreds of millions of elders — no central agency can watch them all; it has to be observed jointly by communities, neighbors, children, and smart devices
- **Food and supply-chain provenance**: the true journey of every batch of goods can only be recorded by the people involved at each step
- **Environmental monitoring**: water quality, air, biodiversity — scattered across every river and forest, can only be sampled by distributed sensors plus volunteers
- **Carbon accounting and ESG**: the real emissions and impact of a company can only be observed by multiple parties, otherwise the self-reported numbers are watered down
- **Grassroots education and healthcare quality**: official statistics will always have structural blind spots; real quality has to be jointly assessed by students, parents, and patients as independent observers
- **Cultural heritage and local memory**: the things scattered across every village and old building — no centralized archive can hold them; only locals, recording continuously, can

The common thread: **all of these belong to the territory where "centralized construction" is already finished and "distributed maintenance and assessment" has just begun**.

Our generation may well be living through an era shift: **from the era of "concentrating forces to do great things," into the era of "distributed forces protecting great things."** The former runs on organizational intensity; the latter runs on observation density. **These two are structurally different things — using the previous era's paradigm to do this era's work is almost guaranteed to fail.**

## The same thing behind all of these scenarios

Writing this far, I've gotten clearer for myself. These scenarios look totally unrelated — from one person's résumé to a crack in a bridge, from a single livestream tip to a country's maintenance system — but they're all doing the same thing:

**Taking the right to collapse fact, originally monopolized by a single central observer, and distributing it to all the people who actually participated in this thing.**

- In recruiting, what gets distributed is the right to collapse "is this person reliable"
- In livestreaming, what gets distributed is the right to collapse "what is this content worth"
- In companies, what gets distributed is the right to collapse "what did this person do this year"
- In OPC, what gets distributed is the right to collapse "who contributed how much in this project"
- In great maintenance, what gets distributed is the right to collapse "what state is this asset in now"
- In eldercare, environment, education, carbon accounting… what gets distributed is, in every case, some kind of fact "centralization used to call the shots on, but in fact couldn't see fully"

The business domain differs, the object being distributed differs, but **the structural transformation is the same**.

This makes me believe one thing: decentralization's landing forms across different industries look wildly different, but at heart they are different projections of the same single move. Once you've grasped that underlying structure, you stop getting stuck on surface questions like "do we issue a token" or "do we go on-chain" — those are just tools. **What actually decides whether a decentralized solution holds is whether it has truly distributed observation rights.**

## The hardest part of landing this isn't actually technology

Last thing I want to say — and it might make people uncomfortable.

The biggest obstacle to this whole structure has never been technology. Chains, protocols, compute, tokens — engineering-wise these are mature. The biggest obstacle is **whether people are willing to take on the responsibility of "being an observer."**

The greatest convenience of centralized structure is that **it lets most people not have to observe, not have to judge, not have to bear**. Platforms screen information for you; bosses define value for you; middlemen build trust for you. You only need to be a passive consumer of pre-collapsed results. That comfort is real comfort.

Decentralization asks you to give up that comfort, in exchange for the right to participate in shaping reality. But participation has cognitive cost — you have to spend time observing, take responsibility for your own judgment, bear the consequences of "what if I got it wrong."

Many decentralized projects fail not because the technology is bad, but **because they overestimated ordinary people's willingness to be observers**.

So if I had to close this piece in one sentence:

**For distributing observation rights, technology only does 30% of the work. The other 70% is educating every participant: you're not a user of this ecosystem — you are one of its collapse nodes, and what the facts look like has something to do with you.**

This road will be slow. But the slower it is, the more it's worth starting now.
